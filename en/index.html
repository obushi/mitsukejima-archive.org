Mitsukejima, a symbol of Suzu City, Ishikawa Prefecture, suffered a major collapse during the 2024 Noto earthquake.
This is a participatory project to track its changes through everyone's photos and preserve them for the future.

What’s happening at Mitsukejima now

The Noto Peninsula earthquake on January 1, 2024 caused strong shaking (seismic intensity of JMA 6+) in Suzu City, Ishikawa Prefecture, where Mitsukejima is located. The photo on the left was taken before the earthquake (Nov 2023), and the photo on the right shows the island after the earthquake (Aug 2025). Move the slider to compare the changes.

According to a project team led by Ishikawa Prefectural University, about 20% of the island’s volume collapsed[1]. But this is not “the end.” The landslide slope increased the island’s total area by about 80%[1], and daily changes in vegetation have begun on the newly exposed slope. While dead trees stand out on the summit, new sprouts have also been found[2, 3], and forest regeneration is progressing[4, 5].

Record change with a time-lapse

Slow change can be hard to notice when you see it every day. By bringing together everyone’s photos and connecting the record over time, we will create time-lapse videos that visualize Mitsukejima’s changes.

Regeneration record

Trees on Mitsukejima may regenerate over the next 5 to 10 years[3]. We will record how the greenery returns and document the island’s resilience, including fixed-point observations of the slope’s seasonal changes.

Photo collection

From a large set of photos with different colors and compositions, we use image processing to extract “change over time” and generate time-lapse footage. Because Mitsukejima is changing rapidly, we need a huge number of photos—your help is essential.

Time-lapse mining

In 2015, a paper was published on methods to generate time-lapse videos from internet photos[6]. We will refer to such approaches and work on generating time-lapses based on the photos we collect.

Building 3D scenes

In addition to time-lapse videos, we will also record Mitsukejima as 3D data using a technique that reconstructs 3D scenes from aerial footage (3D Gaussian Splatting[7]). A similar precedent aimed at “digital reconstruction” is the “OUR Shurijo: Shuri Castle Digital Reconstruction,” launched after the 2019 Shuri Castle fire[8]. In this project, we use drone footage.
